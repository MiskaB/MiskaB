Learning Fast, Working Globally, Few Thoughts on Ecosystem-Driven Business

December 4, 2025

I have dreamt of becoming a digital nomad for a significant part of my life. For me, it's a way to expand perspective while continuing to add real value in my work.

Having lived and worked in the Netherlands, Finland, Ireland and Argentina, one thing becomes obvious very quickly: cultures, both corporate and national, deeply influence how people collaborate and interact. Sometimes the differences between two EMEA countries are bigger than the gap between EMEA and the US. Understanding these nuances shapes how you build trust, resolve conflict, and align with partners and customers.

In partnerships, this contextual awareness can genuinely become a superpower.

Why Fast Learning Matters More Than Deep Expertise

I don't claim to be an expert in every technical field. Far from it. These days I rarely get access to anything that could actually break or generate a serious invoice. I work with test environments that have limits, and I don't have access to production directories. But I do understand how to learn quickly when it matters.

This website is a good example. What started as a simple personal page has turned into an ongoing project with new features, experiments, and soon even persistent memory for returning visitors, games, leaderboards and more. Curiosity creates momentum, and momentum builds capability.

I will use DevSecOps as an example, it's not because I'm an expert or pretending to be one. It's simply to illustrate how I would approach becoming knowledgeable in a complex, multi-vendor domain with many partners involved.

Example Framework: How I Would Approach a DevSecOps Business/Provider Ecosystem

Start with platform, industry and context fundamentals

Understand the security, development and deployment models across major cloud platforms and providers. Not to master every detail, but to know how they differ, where risks emerge and how customers build on top of them. Hyperscaler case studies alone offer a crash course in modern architectures.

Map the ecosystem instead of memorising tools

Almost every domain has somewhat clear categories or solution areas. In DevSecOps, for example, concepts like code security, artifact management, threat detection, workload protection, governance and automation often appear.

From these, you need to understand:

  • what problems each category solves
  • how organisations typically combine them
  • where friction or gaps tend to appear

Vendors will change. Categories rarely do. Sometimes new categories may emerge, or existing ones split into niche areas where for example specific partners excel, hence creating Niche Partners or Specialized Boutiques.

Operate with a few core rules

These guide how I engage with partners and colleagues across cross-functional work.

Transparency

Be explicit about the outcomes we want, the goals we're chasing and the ambitions behind them.

Clear communication

Clarity isn't the same as directness. Directness without clarity is just rude. Clarity means stating what we are strong at, where we're not strong, and where we need alignment or help.

Responsibility

Agree early on who owns what, in what order and in which situations. If this isn't defined up front, things get messy later and people start stepping on each other's toes.

These principles tend to work across any ecosystem because partnerships aren't linear and almost never zero-sum. If a partnership operates as a zero-sum game, it usually isn't a solid foundation to begin with.

Priorities shift. Roadmaps change. AI accelerates everything, especially technology and partnerships. If you can't adjust, shift focus and prioritise accordingly, you can't lead.

Why These Skills Travel Between Ecosystems

Partner ecosystems share similar mechanics whether you're in DevOps, DevSecOps, cloud, AI, SaaS, distributor models or ISV business.

You nearly always need to understand how customers assemble solutions, align value across multiple tools or technologies and balance commercial and technical priorities.

I've moved from IBM subscription services to distribution, then to software management, cloud infrastructure and now software development. The context changed, the roles shifted, but the logic stayed the same.

So Why the Nomadic Life?

People often ask what drives me toward this fairly nomadic way of working. For me, working globally isn't about escapism. It's about gaining more lenses through which to interpret behaviour, business priorities and collaboration styles.

When you've experienced different ways of working — from Nordic solitude and singularity to Dutch pragmatism and directness to Latin American relationship-driven business culture — you don't just manage partnerships. You understand them.

This mindset has driven me forward for a long time. It's about continuing to build adaptable, culturally aware and ecosystem-centric ways of creating value in a world developing faster than ever with AI.
