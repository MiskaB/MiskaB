AI and Leadership: The Real Risk Is Not AI, It's How You Use It

April 1, 2026
AI in combination with modern leadership practices can be a powerful advantage or a fast track to long-term failure.

I can see that many companies are making the same move: cutting floor staff, reducing middle management layers, and calling it "AI-driven productivity." Surely this looks good on paper, yes as you have a lot smaller number in cost column. In reality, it's often a short-term gain and this will leave company very fragile in the future, not to mention the disturbance created in the process.

I think that this is a productivity trap or a delusion. Fewer people combined with AI does not automatically mean better, faster and greater outcomes. What it often leads to is loss of tacit knowledge, slower innovation, and burnout among the people who actually do remain with the company.

This is not a new idea either. In Humanocracy by Hamel and Zanini (2020), the core argument is that organizations create more value by removing bureaucracy and empowering people, not by stripping them out of decision making. I think this becomes even more relevant in the age of AI, or not relevant but paramount. Highly recommend this book: Humanocracy 2020.

The core issue is not AI, it is the leadership decisions around AI and how to use it.

AI does not remove work what it actually does is that it redistributes power, in many ways actually. No longer does floor level person need to wait for top manager or staff engineer to show how something works, or explain processes or concepts. They have all the knowledge in their finger tips. Now what is interesting is that this also means a lot of redistribution of power in terms of traditional hierarchical way. In good empowered teams, the decisions that used to sit at the top of organizations can now be pushed closer to the individuals actually doing the work. This changes the role of leadership entirely.

Middle management is not obsolete. But it does need to evolve. The role shifts from control, reporting and such to enablement, coaching and removing blockers (to use AI!).

Bad leadership uses AI to cut costs. Good leadership uses AI to increase capabilities.
Instead of asking, "How many people can we remove?", the better question is: "How much more can our existing teams achieve if we empower them with AI and autonomy?"

Organizations that get this right are not becoming stronger, they are becoming flatter, faster, and more adaptive. They operate with small, autonomous teams that act more like internal startups than traditional departments. Internal teams support each other, move really fast and fail even faster and go to another solution. They do not have central governance or traditional hierarchy.

Before I get 404 comments on same topics, so yes; these teams need to abide with laws, regulations and be secure by design, which does require certain amount of regulation, tooling, governance and bureaucracy, but the rest... not.

These teams should be empowered to make decisions, experiment, and deliver value without heavy layers of approvals. AI becomes a force multiplier, not a replacement strategy. These teams own the budget, know their industry and/or geographical location and/or customer segments.

In contrast, organizations that focus purely on headcount reduction risk creating rigid structures where fewer people carry more responsibility, innovation slows down, and dependency on processes increases. That may look efficient in a quarterly report, but it is like not going to hold over time. Remains to be seen.

The real question every leader should be asking is simple: Are we using AI to remove people (and cost!), or are we using it to unlock their real potential?

Because the answer to that question will define whether your organization becomes more resilient or more fragile.

AI won't kill companies. Bad leadership using AI will.
